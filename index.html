02 — Featured Project

PROJECT F.E.N.I.X

Narrative Design

Systemic Narrative & Information Architecture

World Context File: FNX-001

Set in a near-future city struggling through a prolonged epidemic, Project F.E.N.I.X puts the player in the role of a government field agent — tasked with containing a mutating biological threat while navigating institutional pressure, fragmented data, and unreliable informants.

The core tension is not the virus itself, but the systems built to manage it: bureaucratic interfaces that obscure as much as they reveal, factions with competing definitions of "containment," and citizens who have learned not to trust official channels. Truth and institutional authority are in constant friction.

Players progress by gathering evidence — field reports, intercepted communications, environmental observation — and constructing their own interpretation of events. The narrative does not reward a single correct reading. It rewards coherent internal logic and the willingness to act on incomplete knowledge.

📓 DESIGNER'S NOTES

Core Concept

Information functions as a gameplay variable. Documents, terminals, and NPC dialogue provide data that players must interpret. Progression is influenced not by combat skill, but by how well the player gathers and processes information.

Narrative Goal

To create a system where Institutional Trust and Player Awareness operate as measurable, interacting variables.

Player behavior (reporting, withholding, investigating independently) directly affects their standing within the institution and the clarity of their understanding of events.

This is not a morality system. It models credibility and access.

Design Pillars

Incomplete Information Players never have full context. Key decisions are made under uncertainty.
Persistent Consequences Information suppressed earlier can reappear later. Trust lost is difficult to regain.
Interface as Narrative Forms, databases, access levels, and redactions function as storytelling tools.
Consistent Faction Logic All factions act according to internal priorities, not according to player morality.

Mission Structure

Each mission contains three layers:

1. Declared Objective Issued by the institution. Always completable.
2. Emergent Objective Discovered through investigation. Requires deeper engagement and typically reduces institutional trust.
3. Personal Cost Consequences of pursuing additional information beyond the sanctioned scope.

Institutional Trust System (Trust vs. Awareness)

Two parallel variables track player behavior:

Trust Represents institutional confidence in the agent. High Trust → resource access, protection, promotion. Low Trust → surveillance, restricted access, termination risk.
Awareness Represents how much the player understands systemic manipulation. High Awareness → expanded narrative interpretation, additional dialogue branches. Low Awareness → limited context but greater institutional alignment.

Information gathering (reading reports, accessing databases, investigating locations) increases Awareness but may reduce Trust.

Surface compliance (executing tasks without deeper investigation) increases Trust but limits Awareness. The two variables rarely increase together.

Decision Architecture

Branching is based on information access, not binary moral choices. The game remains fully completable without deep investigation. However:

Deep Investigation ↓ Trust  ↑ Awareness → Surveillance / Termination Risk
Surface Compliance ↑ Trust  ↓ Awareness → Promotion / Institutional Stability

Players who gather more information face clearer decision contexts. Players who ignore optional content operate with higher uncertainty. Information modifies difficulty and narrative clarity rather than locking endings.

No path is labeled correct. Each reflects a different relationship with the system.

04 — Writing

Writing Samples

FOCUS: Environmental storytelling, tension building, sensory atmosphere

The stairs led down. With each step, it grew colder. Instead of dampness, frost appeared on the walls.

When I reached the top step, for a moment I didn't know whether the sight or the temperature struck me more.

Clotted blood strewn across the floor. Around it were remnants of flesh, bones, and hair. Shreds of clothing were the only evidence that they belonged to humans. In several places, bones were arranged in clusters resembling birds' nests. Fragments of fabric lay within.

I put my hand to my mouth, trying to suppress the urge to vomit.

I'd seen a lot. I'd been through more. But this?

I glanced toward the stairs. Two officers were already fleeing. One had tripped over a standing box. The Styrofoam lid cracked, and pieces of dry ice scattered across the floor. Thick steam began to rise from within.

The cold wasn't an accident.

I took a step back.

In places, the walls showed signs of something trying to escape from the small room next door. The door lay ripped from its frame. Inside, desks were scattered, monitors shattered, keyboard keys scattered like teeth.

The silence was the worst.

There were no screams.

There was no movement.

Only the cold and what looked like a carefully prepared feeding ground.

This time, I left without checking everything.

And for the first time in a long time, I had no rational explanation.

FOCUS: Dialogue-driven scene, emotional tension, character dynamics

"Police! Hands up!"

The door opened with a soft creak. The room was surprisingly intact — desks in their places, a half-finished cup of coffee, a computer on.

I heard a rustle. I raised my gun.

"Come out with your hands up. Don't make me repeat myself."

A little girl slowly emerged from behind the desk. Eight years old, maybe younger. A light dress stained with blood.

I holstered my gun.

"Hey. Relax. I won't hurt you."

She took a step back.

I held up my hands, showing they were empty.

"See? I'm not moving."

I nodded to the officer behind me. He left without a word. I sat down on the floor, leaving a safe distance between us.

"What's your name? I'm Victor."

She was silent for a moment, as if testing if she could trust me.

"Mia... that's what Mommy named me. After her grandmother."

"Pretty name."

I remembered a teddy bear I found in the garden.

"I found Octavia outside. Is that your teddy bear?"

Her eyes lit up immediately.

"Octavia?!"

"Yes. Do you want to see her?"

She nodded without hesitation.

"My friend is waiting outside the door. He'll take you to a safe place. Your teddy bear will be there too."

The girl moved toward me, no longer afraid.

"Thank you!"

She paused only for a moment at the door, then ran out into the hallway.

The room fell silent again.

One word was enough to make her believe that everything would be alright.

It's a shame the world doesn't work that way.

05 — Methodology

Design Process

I start by creating a plot or quest idea. Then I write down my main idea. Once I have that, I add various choices and endings. Then I begin to switch from prose to the player's perspective. I had to learn this method because, as a teenager, I had to create everything from scratch, which turned out to be a huge advantage because I have a good understanding of various topics.

Writing on Wattpad, I received regular feedback (not always pleasant), but it gave me the opportunity to improve my skills.

Also, thanks to writing on my own, I learned to shorten projects because, unfortunately, very often, when I was developing the idea, something seemed like the perfect solution, but during the process of creation, it turned out to be unrealistic.
